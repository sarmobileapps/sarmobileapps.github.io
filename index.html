Twitter Bots

To all users of Twitter and the general social media population, here's some news for you...

What is a Twitter Bot?

A 'Twitter bot' is an account that is controlled by a bot, and a bot is an application that executes an automated task.

At its core, its function is to "tweet and retweet content for specific goals on a larger scale," according to digital security company, Norton.


Tweets by YakuzaFriday

Bots are a tool... for good and bad.

The good:

  • They're often harmless, and will tweet regularly and predictably.
  • Some broadcast important information such as emergency weather alerts.
  • Some tweet about random llama facts every day.

The bad:

  • When a bot is designed to be as human-like as possible for the purpose of manipulation, you have a problem on your hands.
  • These bots can “alter perceptions of political discourse on social media, spread misinformation, or manipulate online rating and review systems” (Pew Research Center, 2018).
  • And according to a Pew Research Center study of 1.2 million tweets sent over a six-week period in Summer 2017, 66% of those tweets were estimated to be bots.
  • Additionally, they found that “[a]bout nine-in-ten (89%) tweeted links to popular news aggregation sites were posted by bots, not human users.”


Why are bots a problem?

"Bad" bots are a problem and we have three primary reasons:

  1. Their effect on politics and internet discourse in general
  2. Their effect on privacy and security
  3. Their effect on wallets (scams)


What have these bots done to the political sphere?

Bots either individually or in groups such as the botnet: “a broad network of automated accounts that work together to appear legitimate, liking and following each other as if they were real” (Norton).

But all malicious bots rely on stealth.

With stealth, bots try to persuade people into certain political beliefs with false information, and it has real world consequences on our politics on perception of the world.

There are a plethora of topics we could jump into, but to preface any conversation, here's a clip of interviews of both Renée Diresta, technical research manager at the Stanford Internet Observatory, and North Atlantic Treaty Organization (NATO) Strategic Communications Center member, Sebastian Bay. They discuss the selling of bots on the black market as well as the concepts of social media manipulation, and “meta” manipulation.



When we remember the COVID-19 pandemic of the abhorrent year that was 2020, there was lots being said about the virus, quarantine, and more online. But did we ever consider that perhaps more than half of the accounts spreading potentially false information were actually not human? According to researchers at Carnegie Mellon University, of the 200 million tweets they found discussing the virus between January and May 2020, 45% were tweeted from accounts that were analyzed to be more bot-like (A.K.A. automated) than human.

Over 100 false narratives about COVID-19 were being disseminated on Twitter via bots. What were these narratives? Tin-foil hat conspiracies like the 5G towers spreading corona, hospitals being filled with mannequins, and similar brands of paranoid.

How have these bots affected data security and privacy?

You can't have a complete discussion about bots on social media without talking about suspicious links, trojans, and malware. Unfortunately, Twitter bots are not an exception.

We mentioned “botnets” earlier, and they're not only great for misinformation in the political sphere, but they're also great for spreading extremely dubious, usually shortened links that could nab your IP address, access your Twitter account, and potentially reach your bank information if they have the means to do so.

But getting your computer hijacked is a pretty extreme case. The possibility of getting scammed, however, is sadly not.


Yes, the classic risk of being scammed. Now even more automated.

Believe it or not, Twitter users like @stimmyskye have been finding that there exist Twitter bots whose sole purpose is to clone other accounts midst potential PayPal and Venmo transactions in order to fool the spender into clicking a fraudulent link.

Quite literally, terms such as “PayPal” and “Venmo” seem to attract these bots.

So how has Twitter changed as a result of these bots?

Bots have become such a problem that Twitter has actively been making attempts to remove them and disallow them from joining Twitter in the first place. But it's not easy to avoid catching human accounts in the crossfire or to create countermeasures.




Twitter is aware of these malicious bots and often relies on artificial intelligence to spot these bots. According to Twitter's official blog, the “Common Thread:”

“The first way the integrity team sniffs out bots is through machine learning. They train algorithms to recognize common patterns of malicious activity; these automatically challenge between 5 to 10M accounts a week. The second, and perhaps more important line of defense, is a forensic team of investigators. These are real people whose job it is to figure out the more complicated questions of whether an account is automated, a real person being paid to behave like a bot, or a real person who just resembles a bot.”

In regards to the users of Twitter, there has certainly been heightened awareness of the existence of bots. If you ever see tweets that censor terms such as “commission,” “NFT,” “PayPal,” and other often monetary/monetarily-related or inflammatory terms, it is likely censored with the forward slash or numbers to avoid attracting bots.

But more positively speaking, there are plenty of fun and harmless bots for pop culture, self-care, and other hobbies. And Twitter does encourage what they believe to be bots that “build solutions!” Do check the link out if you're looking to make a good bot for the world.

How do you spot a bot?

While bots do aim to be as human-like as possible, there are ways to spot them. Norton gives us seven red flags:

  1. The potential Twitter bot shares the same IP adress as other potiential bots.
  2. The timing of tweets is pretty close together.
  3. Replies seem to be automatic.
  4. Somehow, two things were tweeted at almost the same time.
  5. The account is new.
  6. The twitter handle contains numbers, and there's no profile picture (or there's a stolen one. Reverse image searching is recommended here).
  7. It's retweeting and tweeting at an inhuman speed, and lacks followers but follows lots of accounts.*

    8. *It's worth noting that some bots manage to garner thousands of followers despite being bots, as some previous videos have illustrated.


What can you do when you find a bot that's obviously malicious?


Well here's Norton's suggestion:

"You can find browser plugins that look at indicators to help identify whether or not a Twitter account is a bot. These third-pary tools can help flag suspicious accounts so you won't be fooled. Whether you use a third-party tool or learn to recognize bots yourself, it's smart to understand how automated platforms can enable the mass spread of false information. Awareness and caution can help. It's a good idea to watch for red flags and be skeptical about information contained in tweets. Go to reputable sources for fact-checking. And avoid clicking on links in tweets that could be embedded with malware."

All in all, bots are not evil. They are a tool, and as resident expert at the Atlantic Council's Digital Forensic Research Lab in the United Kingdom Ben Nimmo wonderfully said:

"Bots don't exist on their own. They don't exist in a vacuum. They're part of a bigger ecosystem. So we need to be aware of bots, we need to be aware of the role they play, but we shouldn't be exaggerating them. Bots can be amplifiers, people can use them to make an unpopular opinion look more popular. But ultimately, they are only one part of the problem and to keep the whole ecosystem healthy is one of the big challenges of the next few years."
